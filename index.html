01 / Current research

When a model learns a fact in one language, what changes in the others?

Modern editing methods can rewrite a factual association inside a language model without retraining it. Yet an edit made in English may disappear when the same fact is queried in French, Spanish, Italian, or Chinese.

At KU Leuven’s LIIR lab, within the INCLINE research programme, I investigate this failure through activation patching, controlled ablations, multilingual evaluation, and representation-level interventions. The central distinction is simple but consequential: is the new knowledge missing, or is it stored in the model but inaccessible to the foreign-language query?

This question connects a practical reliability problem to a deeper one: knowing that information is encoded somewhere is not the same as showing that the model can use it when it matters.

02 / The longer arc

What I want to understand next.

I’m interested in the point where an internal representation becomes a behavior—and in building evidence strong enough to tell the difference between correlation, control, and genuine model use.

A

Mechanistic interpretability

Finding causal mechanisms behind model behavior, with a clear separation between what can be decoded, what can be steered, and what the model actually relies on.

B

Reliable interventions

Designing editing, steering, and training-time mechanisms that remain precise outside the narrow setting in which they were built.

C

Social cognition in LLMs

Understanding how models represent beliefs, stance, persuasion, and other agents—and how those mechanisms can become more interpretable and controllable.

Across all three: cross-lingual transfer is not an afterthought; it is a test of whether a claimed mechanism is genuinely general.
